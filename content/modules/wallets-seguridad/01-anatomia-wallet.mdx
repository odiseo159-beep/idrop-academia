## A wallet is not where your money lives

This is the single most important sentence in this module:

> Your tokens live on the **blockchain**. Your wallet just holds the **keys** that prove you control them.

If your phone falls in the ocean, your tokens are still on the chain. If you can recover your **seed phrase**, you can install any compatible wallet on any device, restore the same keys, and access the same tokens. The wallet is a tool. The keys are the asset.

This sounds obvious, but a huge percentage of newcomer mistakes — sending support requests like *"I uninstalled MetaMask, did I lose my BNB?"* — come from mixing up the wallet (the app) with the keys (the secret).

## Keys, addresses, and how they relate

Every wallet on BNB Chain (and Ethereum, and many others) is built on the same cryptographic primitive: an **elliptic-curve key pair** called secp256k1.

Three things are involved, in this order:

1. **Private key** — a 256-bit random number. The secret. If anyone learns this, they can move your funds.
2. **Public key** — derived from the private key by elliptic-curve math. Safe to share.
3. **Address** — the last 20 bytes of the keccak256 hash of the public key. This is what looks like `0x742d35Cc6634C0532925a3b844Bc9e7595f0bEb1`. Safe to share publicly.

The math is one-way: from a private key you can always derive the public key and the address. From an address, deriving the private key is computationally infeasible — that's what makes the system work.

A signed transaction is just a message ("send 1 BNB from 0xABC to 0xDEF") combined with a cryptographic signature produced by the private key. Anyone can verify the signature using the public key, which proves the owner of the private key authorized the message.

## Seed phrase: the master key

You don't usually back up a single private key — you back up a **seed phrase** (also called a *mnemonic* or *recovery phrase*). It looks like this:

```
witch collapse practice feed shame open despair creek road again ice least
```

That's 12 words from a fixed dictionary of 2048 words. Sometimes 24 words. The standard is called **BIP-39**.

From those 12 words you can deterministically derive an effectively unlimited number of private keys. So one seed phrase backs up your "Account 1", "Account 2", "Account 3" in MetaMask — they are all derived from that single seed using a derivation path.

Practical consequences:

- **Lose your seed phrase → lose access to every account derived from it.** No one can help you. There is no support line.
- **Anyone who learns your seed phrase has total control of every account derived from it.** They can drain everything in seconds.

This is why the seed phrase is *the* thing you protect. Your antivirus won't save you. Your bank won't save you. The chain doesn't care.

## EOA vs Contract Account

When you create a wallet in MetaMask, you get an **Externally Owned Account (EOA)** — an address controlled by a private key. That's the default and what 99% of users have.

The other type is a **contract account** — an address controlled by smart contract code rather than a private key. **Smart wallets** (also called account-abstracted wallets) like Safe, Argent, or Biconomy fall into this category. They can do things EOAs can't: multiple signers, spending limits, social recovery. But they're a more advanced topic.

For BNB Chain in 2025, start with an EOA. You can graduate to smart wallets later when you have more value at stake or specific requirements.

## What you actually carry

After all of this, here's the mental model:

- **Address (`0x...`)** — your public identity. Share freely.
- **Private key / Seed phrase** — the secret. Share with no one. Ever.
- **Wallet app (MetaMask, Rabby, Trust)** — a key manager + UI. Replaceable.
- **Tokens** — entries on the blockchain pointing to your address. You don't "store" them.
