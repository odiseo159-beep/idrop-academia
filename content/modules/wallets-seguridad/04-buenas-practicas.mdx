## A simple, durable security routine

You don't need a paranoid setup. You need a *consistent* one. Here's a routine experienced users converge on. Adapt the threshold values to your own situation.

### Tier the wallets

Use at least two, ideally three, separate wallets:

| Wallet | Backed by | Holds | Use for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cold / Vault** | Hardware wallet (Ledger, Trezor) | Long-term holdings, anything you'd be devastated to lose | Receiving from exchanges, long-term storage. Rarely connect to dApps. |
| **Hot / Daily** | MetaMask, Rabby, etc. | Working balance for routine activity | Swaps on PancakeSwap, lending on Venus, anything you've used before. |
| **Burner** | Fresh MetaMask account | Tiny balance, refreshed often | Trying new dApps, claiming airdrops, anything experimental. |

Each tier has its own seed (well, the hot/burner can be different accounts under the same seed — but consider separate seeds for true isolation). The whole point is that a compromise of the burner can never reach the vault.

### Back up the seed properly

For the cold wallet:

- **Pen and paper** for the immediate backup, stored somewhere only you can access. Not your desk drawer.
- **Steel backup** (Cryptosteel, Billfodl, or DIY washer plates) for fire/water resistance once the holdings justify the cost (~$50-100).
- **Geographic separation** if you're holding life-changing amounts: two copies in two different physical locations.

Never:

- Photograph the seed.
- Type the seed into any device that's online except the wallet that produced it.
- Store the seed digitally without strong, offline encryption (and even then, the encrypted file is a target).

### Use a hardware wallet for meaningful value

The "meaningful value" threshold is personal. For most people, anything above what you'd lose without complaining warrants hardware. Two reasons:

- **Phishing-resistant signing**: even on a fully compromised computer, you cannot sign a transaction without physically pressing buttons on the device. The device shows the destination address and amount on its own screen — you verify against that, not your monitor.
- **Cold storage**: the hardware wallet can hold the seed indefinitely without ever connecting to the internet. The signing operations happen on demand.

A Ledger or Trezor costs roughly $80 retail. Buy directly from the manufacturer's website, not Amazon (supply chain attacks have happened). Set it up yourself; never use a "pre-configured" device.

### Read every popup

This is the discipline most people drop after a few weeks of using web3.

Every MetaMask popup that asks you to sign something has, somewhere, a "function" being called. Common ones:

- **`transfer` / `transferFrom`**: actually moving tokens. Should match what you intended.
- **`approve` / `permit`**: granting a contract permission to spend your tokens. Pay attention to *which* contract and *what amount*. Unlimited approvals on stablecoins are a long-standing risk.
- **`swap`**: routine for DEX activity.
- **An opaque hex blob**: this is a custom contract call. If you can't read it and don't trust the dApp, don't sign.

If the popup looks unusual for what the website claims you're doing, **stop and check**. A 30-second pause has saved more wallets than any tool.

### Use simulation tools

Browser extensions like **Pocket Universe**, **Wallet Guard**, or **Blockaid** (built into Rabby and rolling out in MetaMask) intercept transactions and show you a plain-language preview of the *outcome*: "After this transaction, your balance will be: -1.0 BNB, -50 USDT, +0 of anything." If the preview reveals an unexpected outflow, you have a clear cancel signal.

This is the single highest-leverage tool against drainers in 2025.

### Audit approvals periodically

Every couple of months, visit **revoke.cash** with your hot wallet connected. Look at what addresses have spending power over your tokens. Anything you don't actively use — revoke. The revocation transaction costs gas, but a stale approval to a forgotten contract is a permanent attack surface.

### Have a recovery plan

Ask yourself, calmly, before anything happens:

- If my phone disappeared right now, can I recover my hot wallet from a different device using my seed?
- If I were incapacitated, would someone I trust be able to recover the cold wallet, and is that the right outcome for them?
- Where are my seed backups, exactly? Could I describe their locations to my own future self if my memory failed?

These aren't morbid — they're operational. A wallet you can't recover is functionally identical to a wallet that's been stolen.

## You're now beyond the danger zone

Most onchain newcomers lose money in the first six months because they didn't know any of this. You now do. The amount of risk you take from here on is a function of your own choices — which is the right place to be.

You're ready for the actual interesting parts of the chain: **DeFi**, **tokens**, **NFTs**, and how launchpads work. Those are the next modules.
