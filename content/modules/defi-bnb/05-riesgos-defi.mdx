## The four ways you lose money in DeFi

Every dollar lost in DeFi falls into one of these buckets:

1. **Impermanent loss / price action**: the assets you held went down, or your LP position underperformed simple holding.
2. **Smart contract exploits**: a bug or design flaw lets an attacker drain the protocol.
3. **Operational mistakes**: wrong network, wrong token contract, malicious approvals — the entire previous module.
4. **Rug pulls**: the team intentionally exits with user funds.

Each has different mitigations. Let's go through them.

## Impermanent loss with real numbers

Let's redo the example from lesson 3 more carefully.

You deposit into a CAKE/BNB pool when:
- 1 CAKE = $2
- 1 BNB = $600
- You deposit 100 CAKE + 0.333 BNB = $400 total

Now consider three price scenarios after one month:

**Scenario A — both unchanged**:
Pool composition for your share: still 100 CAKE + 0.333 BNB. Value: $400. **IL: $0**.

**Scenario B — CAKE doubles, BNB unchanged** (CAKE = $4, BNB = $600):
The AMM rebalances by selling some of your CAKE for BNB.
Your share becomes ~70.7 CAKE + 0.471 BNB = ~$565.
Holding instead: 100 CAKE × $4 + 0.333 BNB × $600 = $600.
**IL: -$35 (-5.7%)**.

**Scenario C — CAKE goes to zero**:
Arbitrageurs drain BNB out of the pool buying CAKE all the way down. Your share is now ~all CAKE, worth $0.
Holding instead: 100 CAKE × $0 + 0.333 BNB × $600 = $200.
**IL: -$200 (catastrophic)**.

The asymmetry is the lesson: **IL hurts most when one asset moves dramatically against you in the wrong direction**. For volatile pairs, IL can dwarf the fees you earn.

For stable pairs (USDT/USDC), IL is essentially zero because the prices barely deviate. That's why stable pools dominate large institutional LP capital.

The simple heuristic:

> **The more correlated the two tokens, the lower your IL risk.**

CAKE/BNB has low correlation (different tokens, different drivers). USDT/USDC has very high correlation (both pegged to $1). BTCB/WBTC have near-perfect correlation (both wrapped versions of BTC).

## Smart contract exploits

These are the headline-grabbing $100M+ losses you read about. Categories:

- **Logic bugs**: the contract does something the developers didn't intend. (Example: not validating a price feed, allowing manipulation.)
- **Reentrancy**: an attacker calls into the contract recursively before it can update state, draining funds. Less common today but historically devastating.
- **Oracle manipulation**: protocols that price assets off DEX spot prices can be tricked by flash-loan-funded price manipulation.
- **Cross-chain bridge exploits**: bridges hold huge amounts of locked assets and have repeatedly been the biggest single targets.
- **Governance attacks**: an attacker accumulates enough governance tokens to vote in malicious changes.

There's no foolproof defense against these. Mitigations:

- **Time-tested protocols**. A protocol that's held >$100M for >2 years without an exploit has been more thoroughly battle-tested than any audit can simulate.
- **Multiple audits from reputable firms**. CertiK, Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, Quantstamp. One audit is OK; multiple from different firms is better.
- **Active bug bounty programs**. Immunefi-listed bounties of $100k-$10M signal the team takes security seriously.
- **Diversification**. Don't put all your DeFi exposure into one protocol no matter how trustworthy it seems.

## Operational mistakes

Already covered in the Wallets module. Quick refresher:

- Verify token contracts on BscScan before swapping. Many "USDT" listings on PancakeSwap are scam tokens with the same name.
- Read every approval popup. Set spending limits to exactly what you need rather than infinite.
- Use a separate "DeFi" wallet from your savings.
- Audit your approvals at revoke.cash periodically.

## Rug pulls

A rug pull is when the team or a malicious actor exits with user funds. Variants:

- **Liquidity rug**: the team is the only LP. They withdraw their LP, dropping the pool to zero. Your tokens are now unsellable.
- **Mint rug**: the contract has a hidden mint function that the owner uses to inflate supply, sell, and dump.
- **Honeypot**: the contract lets you buy but reverts on sells. You can never exit.
- **Slow rug**: the team gradually sells their allocation over months, slowly tanking the price while pretending nothing's wrong.

Red flags that combine to indicate rug risk:

- **Anonymous team** with no track record (many legitimate projects start anon — but combined with the rest, it adds up).
- **No audit**, or audit by a no-name firm.
- **No mint cap** or **owner can mint freely** (check on BscScan: read contract → look for mint, transferOwnership, blacklist).
- **Locked liquidity ≠ fair**. Many "locked LP" tokens are locked for 1 month, not 1 year. Check the lock duration.
- **APRs absurdly higher than competitors** for no obvious reason.
- **Twitter pumping with bot followers**, paid influencer shilling, no real product.

If 3+ of these apply, treat it as a casino bet, not an investment.

## A repeatable due diligence checklist

Before depositing any meaningful amount in a new DeFi protocol:

1. **Read the docs**. If the protocol can't explain what it does in plain language, skip.
2. **Check the audit reports** linked from official sources. Skim for high/critical findings and how they were resolved.
3. **Check TVL history** on DefiLlama. Sustained TVL > $10M for >6 months is meaningful.
4. **Look at the contract on BscScan**. Verified source? Owner address? Recent changes? Unusual functions?
5. **Search "[protocol name] hack" and "[protocol name] rug"** on Twitter and Discord. If you find drama, read it.
6. **Test with a small amount first**. Deposit $50, withdraw it. If withdrawals work cleanly, scale up gradually.
7. **Set a stop-loss policy**. Know in advance what would make you exit (TVL drop > 30%, governance change, etc.) and follow it.

This is boring, slow work. But "boring and slow" is how people who survive in DeFi actually make returns over years.
